Democracy and otherness: Rousseau facing the expulsion of the other

2026-06-02 Philosophy

Introduction

Democracy, from the ancient Greek word Demokratia, is the combination of demos which mean "the common place" or "citizens of a specific area" and kratos that express "power" or "rule". In other words Democracy can be translated as "the common place ruled by its citizens". Is is usually understood as political system based in the participation of all people, or the vast majority, in the government of their community. However, this kind of governance raises a deeper question: What social conditions make it possible the democracy? My hypothesis is that from the theory of social contract from Jean-Jacques Rousseau and the contemporary critic from Byung-Chul Han the "expulsion of the other", it is possible to argue that a society dominated by the absolute sameness and homogenization of their individuals losses the required fundamentals in other to develop a democratic live. Democracy depends not merely on the participation of citizens, but on the preservation of alterity among them. When social, economic, or technological forces reduce citizens to expressions of the same preferences and behaviors, democratic deliberation gives way to the administration of conformity. In a society where difference disappears, democratic procedures remain formally intact but lose much of their substantive function, since there are fewer genuine disagreements to deliberate. It may even be replaced by control and repression which objective is to produce uniformity.

Rousseau and the necessity of the other

In "The Social Contract", Rousseau states that the sovereignty resides in the people. The government is representing the general will, but the legitimate political authority comes only from consent of the governed. He argue that to preserve freedom, citizens must rule themself by prioritizing the common good. This is done by the social contract; an action that do not requires to be written nor even needs to formally defined to exist. It consist of the desire of people who identifies themself as part of a community, to cover other members under a common law. This conception suppose the existence of multiple individuals that take participation in construction of laws and political organization.

Democracy is the participation of people in governance; it assumes the existence of citizens different to each other that discuss about what must be done. The laws, institutions and collective decisions arise from the confrontation of ideas between people with different perspectives, beliefs, interest, needs and experiences. In consequence, the concrete form of democratic government is constantly open to discussion and transformation.

How many difficult matters this governance suppose? First, a very small State that make it easy to congregate the people and where each citizen may know easily to everyone else; second, a great simplicity of traditions that avoid multitude of questions and thorny discussions [...] In addition there is not government as subdued to civil wars and internal unrest as the democratic one, because there is no one that tends as strong and as continuously to change of form. He declares that, "were there a people of gods, their government would be democratic. So perfect a government is not for men." Rousseau does not celebrate difference for its own sake. Rather, he seeks a political order in which citizens remain distinct individuals while participating in a common civic project. Democracy therefore requires neither absolute difference nor absolute sameness.

Rousseau asserts that democracy is incompatible with representative institutions. He wrote. "In the ancient republics...the people never had representatives... The moment a people allows itself to be represented, it is no longer free: it no longer exists. Furthermore, according to Rousseau, if a political association that is small enough to practice direct democracy, such as a city-state, were to come into existence, it would inevitably be subjugated by larger nation-states and thereby cease to be democratic.

Although separated by more than two centuries, Rousseau and Han address a common question: what happens to political freedom when citizens cease to relate to each other as genuinely distinct persons? Rousseau feared that social inequality would corrupt the citizen and make genuine political participation impossible. Han fears that digital and economic mechanisms produce conformity by eliminating alterity. Despite their different contexts, both identify social conditions that threaten freedom.

The expulsion of the other

Byung-Chul Han argues that our contemporary society tends to reject the different. The other disappear as alterity and as negativity. The negativity of other gives form or identity, without it the hell of sameness grow until it becomes in an amorphous mass. The other is subdued to utility, economic calculus and evaluation. The dissidence is replaced with the repetition of the sameness.

According to Han, this homogenization does not operate by means of physical coercion, but through social and psychological mechanisms. Individuals are voluntary subdued to dynamics of efficiency that arrive in the reproduction the same values and behavior. To be authentic means be free of preconfigured and imposed expressions and behaviors. The imperative of authenticity force the self to produce itself. It gives birth to a narcissist coercion. The narcissist is blind at the moment to see the other, in consequence the other disappears. To be sure that everyone is forced to this imperative its needed a mechanism of vigilance and coercion. The perfect mechanism is the digital Panoptic. Since It works without perspective, It is more efficient. To the digital Panoptic thoughts are hardly never noticed. Macrodata manage to monitor without necessity to see.

Homogenization as threat to democracy

If democracy requires the existence of citizens with the capacity to argue its differences in the public scenery, the proliferation of sameness weaken its fundamentals. When the alterity disappear, the debate losses its meaning. There no longer exists a true discussion among different perspectives, but the repetition of the same vision of how the world must be ruled.

Democracy then ceases to fulfill its natural function. Political decisions are no longer presented as the result of discussion among diverse citizens, because the necessity to know what most of them think about the community needs, but as the automatic implementation of what everyone supposedly desires. Dissent is no longer perceived as a legitimate part of political life and begins to be viewed as an anomaly that must be corrected. The consequence is a form of repression that differs from that described by classical theories of power. It is not physical violence directed against the body, but rather psychological and social pressure that forces individuals to conform to a common model. Equality thus ceases to be a legal status among citizens and becomes a demand.

The violent power of equal tries to erase singularities that do not want to be subdued to global or general exchange. Cultural homogenisation refers to the process by which local cultures become more similar due to global influences such as media, trade, and migration. Despite these pressures, many places actively resist cultural homogenisation in order to protect local identity, traditions, and diversity. A growing body of scholarship argues that the current wave of nationalism should be understood as a response to globalisation. In this view, nationalism is the reaction to the rapid, often unsettling changes that global integration brings to economics, culture, and politics all at once. People who feel left behind by the disruptions of global markets and unfamiliar cultural change look to national identity as a source of stability and belonging. In that sense, the Nacionalism that woke up among right-wing and identitarian parties are also reactions against the domain of sameness described by Han.

Conclusion

Rousseau's political theory and Byung-Chul Han's social critique help us to understand that democracy requires differences in order to exist. The social contract presupposes a plurality of citizens capable of participating in the formation of the collective will. Conversely, the exclusion of difference reduces that plurality and transforms politics into the management of sameness. Where the other disappears, democracy loses its raison d'être and leads to mechanisms of homogenization that limits freedom.
